# Expected Number of Uniform(0,1) Draws to Exceed 1: e

**Main Takeaway**
When we draw independent Uniform(0, 1) random variables $X_1,X_2,\dots$ and stop at

$$
N \;=\;\min\Bigl\{n:\sum_{i=1}^n X_i>1\Bigr\},
$$

then

$$
\mathbb{E}[N] \;=\;e.
$$

This striking result first appeared in the Soviet literature and was rediscovered in modern contexts, and connects classical stopping‐time theory with renewal processes and integral equations.

## 1. Historical Context

1. **M. I. Yadrenko (1960s)**
Yadrenko observed that adding i.i.d. Uniform(0,1) variables until the partial sum exceeds 1 yields an expected stopping time of $e$. His note lay largely unknown in the West until recent rediscoveries[1].
2. **K. G. Russell (1980s)**
Generalized Yadrenko’s threshold to an arbitrary level $a>0$, deriving closed‐form expressions for the distribution, mean, and variance of

$$
N_a \;=\;\min\Bigl\{n:\sum_{i=1}^n X_i>a\Bigr\}.
$$
3. **Renewal Theory \& Putnam Problems**
The uniform‐sum stopping problem has appeared as a Putnam competition question and on Math StackExchange[2][3], serving as a pedagogical example of integral‐equation methods in stopping‐time analysis.

## 2. Elementary Integral‐Equation Derivation

Define, for $0\le t\le1$,

$$
m(t)\;=\;\mathbb{E}\bigl[N_t\bigr],\quad
N_t=\min\{n:\sum_{i=1}^nX_i>t\}.
$$

Condition on the first draw $X_1=x$:

$$
m(t)
=\;1+\int_0^t m(t-x)\,dx
\;=\;1+\int_0^t m(u)\,du.
$$

Differentiating gives the ODE

$$
m'(t)=m(t),\quad m(0)=1,
$$

so $m(t)=e^t$. In particular,

$$
\mathbb{E}[N]=m(1)=e.
$$

This concise proof is ubiquitous in probabilistic folklore and appears in several online expositions[2].

## 3. Distributional Perspective

One can also show

$$
\mathbb{P}(N=n)
=\mathbb{P}\bigl(S_{n-1}\le1<S_n\bigr)
=\frac{(n-1)}{n!},\quad n\ge2,
$$

where $S_n=\sum_{i=1}^nX_i$.  Then

$$
\mathbb{E}[N]
=\sum_{n=2}^\infty n\cdot\frac{n-1}{n!}
=\sum_{k=1}^\infty\frac{1}{k!}
=e.
$$

## 4. Advanced Mathematical Perspectives

1. **Renewal‐Process Framework**
Viewing $\{X_i\}$ as i.i.d. inter‐arrival times defines a renewal process; $N$ is the first renewal after time 1.  Classical renewal‐theory results then yield exact and asymptotic properties of $N$[3].
2. **Martingale \& Stopping‐Time Techniques**
The uniform‐sum problem illustrates Wald’s identity and optional‐stopping arguments when one constructs suitable martingales (e.g., exponential martingales tied to partial sums) and verifies integrability conditions[4].
3. **Generalized Distributions**
Coḥen et al. (2024) compare first‐passage times for uniform, exponential, and Lévy summands[1].  They derive expressions for $\mathbb{E}[N_a]$ beyond the uniform case, showing how the mean depends on the summand distribution.
4. **Spectral \& Analytic Methods**
The characteristic‐function approach to sums of uniforms leads to Fourier‐series representations of the stopping‐time distribution and connects to the theory of random partitions and combinatorial identities[3].

**Conclusion**
The uniform‐sum stopping problem epitomizes the elegance of stopping‐time analysis: a simple integral equation yields the transcendental constant $e$ as the expected stopping time. Its study bridges elementary probability, renewal theory, and advanced analytical techniques, making it a perennial example in both teaching and research.
